Gamble Fitness

EECS 395 - mHealth - Northwestern University

ABOUT

With recent studies showing that physical activity monitoring devices such as the Fitbit do not actually aid in long-term habit formation or activity increase, new ideas must be brought to the table on how to increase user activity levels within the Quantified Self movement. Habit formation requires intentional thinking by the user as well as behavioral adaptation to sustain and maintain changes in lifestyle. This will empower individuals to not only track their activity levels but also work to improve them over time.

One of the methods being explored currently is the ‘gamification’ of activity tracking, where users have to think about their activity levels and therefore evaluate if they are exerting enough throughout the day in order to ‘win’ the game. By making the game interactive and rewarding, we hope that users will be motivated to develop long-term exercise habits.

Our hypothesis is that ndividuals who are exposed to the gaming strategy of approximating their activity levels will be more engaged in the intervention and remain more physically active as compared to a simple display of fitness information (e.g. Fitbit).
